    <section id="pane-article" class="section active">
      <div class="kicker">Article</div>
      <div class="topic-title">Upcycling an Obsolete Railway Bridge</div>
      <div class="topic-sub">How BIM supports reuse decisions and reduces retrofit uncertainty.</div>

      <div class="note">
        Focus: <span class="kw">upcycling</span>, <span class="kw">as-is modelling</span>, and <span class="kw">constructability</span>.
      </div>

      <p>
        The article presents a sustainable strategy for an obsolete railway bridge: rather than demolish the structure, the team identifies
        components with <span class="kw">residual load-bearing capacity</span> and repurposes them into new public infrastructure.
        The architectural value is not only the object’s form, but the ability to create new pedestrian/cycle connectivity with an already-built span.
      </p>

      <p>
        Retrofit projects are dominated by uncertainty. A key move is building an <span class="kw">as-is model</span> from <span class="kw">reality capture</span>
        (often a <span class="kw">point cloud</span>) so decisions reflect the real geometry and constraints—not idealized drawings.
        This supports coordination, interface detailing, and reliable quantities.
      </p>

      <p>
        BIM supports construction planning through <span class="kw">constructability</span> review (access, staging, lifting, temporary works)
        and coordination through <span class="kw">clash detection</span>. Reuse strengthens the sustainability case by lowering waste and
        <span class="kw">embodied carbon</span>, aligning with <span class="kw">circular economy</span> thinking.
      </p>
    </section>

      <div class="grid2">
        <div class="block">
          <div class="badge red">Reuse logic</div>
          <ul>
            <li><span class="kw">Upcycling</span>: reuse components for higher value than scrap.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Residual capacity</span>: prove what the structure can still do safely.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Embodied carbon</span>: avoid new material emissions by reuse.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Circular economy</span>: keep structural components in long-term use.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
        <div class="block">
          <div class="badge blue">Delivery logic</div>
          <ul>
            <li><span class="kw">As-is model</span>: represent reality, including deviations.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Reality capture</span> / <span class="kw">Point cloud</span>: reliable geometric baseline.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Tolerance management</span>: design for imperfect fit.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Constructability</span>: staging, access, lifting, temporary works.</li>
            <li><span class="kw">Clash detection</span>: find spatial conflicts before site.</li>
          </ul>

      <div class="task">
        <h3>Task 1 — Reuse Feasibility Pitch</h3>
        <div class="meta">
          <span class="pill red">Client meeting</span>
          <span class="pill blue">Concept stage</span>
        </div>
        <p class="prompt">
          The city wants a quick new pedestrian crossing. You have access to a dismantled railway truss segment.
          Draft a 6-point “reuse pitch” for the client: what you propose, what you need to verify, and what risks you manage.
        </p>
        <details>
          <summary>Reveal key</summary>
          <div class="key">
            <ul>
              <li>Define the concept: <span class="kw">upcycling</span> + intended new use (ped/cycle).</li>
              <li>Request evidence: inspections + analysis for <span class="kw">residual capacity</span> and <span class="kw">fatigue</span>.</li>
              <li>Verify geometry using <span class="kw">reality capture</span> → <span class="kw">as-is model</span>.</li>
              <li>Identify constraints: <span class="kw">clearance envelope</span>, access/maintenance, regulations.</li>
              <li>Plan delivery: <span class="kw">constructability</span> (access, lifting, temporary works, staging).</li>
              <li>Make sustainability legible: waste avoided + <span class="kw">embodied carbon</span> benefits.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </details>
      </div>

      <div class="task">
        <h3>Task 2 — As-Is Modelling Strategy</h3>
        <div class="meta">
          <span class="pill red">BIM execution</span>
          <span class="pill blue">Coordination</span>
        </div>
        <p class="prompt">
          The existing records are incomplete. Propose a modelling workflow for the first 2 weeks:
          what you scan, what you model, and how you represent uncertainty/tolerances so contractors can build.
        </p>
        <details>
          <summary>Reveal key</summary>
          <div class="key">
            <ul>
              <li>Week 1: collect <span class="kw">point cloud</span> + targeted survey on critical nodes/connections.</li>
              <li>Build an <span class="kw">as-is model</span> focusing on interfaces, not “everything.”</li>
              <li>Add <span class="kw">tolerance management</span> zones at old↔new interfaces.</li>
              <li>Tag uncertainties (concealed areas, unknown plates/bolts, corrosion) as model issues.</li>
              <li>Run early <span class="kw">clash detection</span> with deck/rail/lighting zones.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </details>
      </div>

      <div class="task">
        <h3>Task 3 — Constructability + Staging Plan</h3>
        <div class="meta">
          <span class="pill red">Construction planning</span>
          <span class="pill blue">Safety</span>
        </div>
        <p class="prompt">
          Site access is tight, the city requires minimal disruption, and lifts must happen during short windows.
          Outline a staging plan (steps) and list the minimum BIM views/deliverables you’d hand to the contractor.
        </p>
        <details>
          <summary>Reveal key</summary>
          <div class="key">
            <ul>
              <li>Define lift windows + access routes + laydown area.</li>
              <li>Model <span class="kw">temporary works</span> zones (supports, lifting frames, exclusion zones).</li>
              <li>Sequence: foundations → supports → lift → lock-in → deck + safety systems.</li>
              <li>Deliverables: stage views, lift diagrams, clearance checks, interface details, clash report.</li>
              <li>Confirm <span class="kw">clearance envelope</span> at each stage, not only final state.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </details>
      </div>

      <div class="task">
        <h3>Task 4 — Sustainability Argument That Holds Up</h3>
        <div class="meta">
          <span class="pill red">Stakeholders</span>
          <span class="pill blue">Sustainability</span>
        </div>
        <p class="prompt">
          A stakeholder says: “Reuse is marketing.” Build a structured argument (5 bullets) showing what you measure,
          what you compare against, and what trade-offs you acknowledge.
        </p>
        <details>
          <summary>Reveal key</summary>
          <div class="key">
            <ul>
              <li>Compare reuse vs new build: materials, time, disruption, cost ranges.</li>
              <li>Quantify <span class="kw">embodied carbon</span>: new steel vs reused steel + refurbishment impacts.</li>
              <li>Acknowledge trade-offs: coatings, repairs, transport, compliance upgrades.</li>
              <li>Connect to <span class="kw">circular economy</span>: component life extension, waste reduction.</li>
              <li>Explain co-benefits: faster delivery + urban connectivity + heritage value.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </details>
